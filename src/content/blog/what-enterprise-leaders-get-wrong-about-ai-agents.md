Every enterprise leadership deck I've seen this year has an AI agents slide. Most of them will not survive contact with production.

I've spent 12+ years deploying conversational AI, chatbots and automation for global workforces, through platform migrations, a historic corporate separation, and the shift from scripted bots to NLP to agentic AI. The technology has changed dramatically. The reasons deployments fail have barely changed at all.

## Mistake 1: Treating the agent as the product

The agent is the interface. The product is the *service outcome*, a resolved question, a completed transaction, a policy explained correctly for the employee's country and business unit.

When teams obsess over the agent (the model, the persona, the demo) and neglect the service catalog behind it, they ship something that chats beautifully and resolves nothing. Users try it twice, fail twice, and never come back. Rebuilding trust after a bad launch is far harder than launching late.

## Mistake 2: Ignoring the knowledge foundation

An AI agent is only as good as the content it can ground itself in. In most large organizations, that content is scattered across portals, PDFs, tribal knowledge and outdated articles with no owner.

Before our chatbot quality program at GE could reduce unresolved utterances by 84%, the biggest wins came not from model improvements but from *content* improvements: rewriting articles for answerability, closing coverage gaps, establishing ownership and review cycles. If you're planning an agentic AI rollout and you haven't audited your knowledge base, you're planning a very expensive way to surface your content debt.

## Mistake 3: No definition of "resolved"

Ask a vendor how their agent performs and you'll hear containment rates. Ask an employee and you'll hear whether they got their problem solved. These are not the same number.

A query can be "contained" (the user didn't reach a human) and completely unresolved (the user gave up). If you measure only containment, your agent will learn to be a very effective dead end. Instrument for actual resolution: did the user complete the task, stop repeating the question, avoid opening a ticket through another channel?

## Mistake 4: Underestimating governance

Enterprise AI agents touch HR policy, payroll, benefits, legal entities in dozens of countries. Every answer is a compliance surface. The unglamorous work of SME validation, access controls, audit trails, versioned content and risk tracking is what separates programs that scale from pilots that get quietly shut down.

This is where agentic AI raises the stakes: an agent that *acts* (opens cases, updates records, triggers workflows) needs the same rigor you'd apply to any system of record integration, plus guardrails for the non-determinism.

## What the successful programs do

The programs I've seen work share a pattern:

1. **They start from demand data.** Top contact drivers, ticket categories, search queries, then automate the highest-volume, lowest-risk journeys first.
2. **They fix the knowledge layer first.** Ownership, quality standards, review cycles, before connecting a model to it.
3. **They measure resolution, not deflection.** And they read the unresolved queries weekly. That's the roadmap, written by your users.
4. **They ship improvement cycles, not launches.** The bot that wins is the one with a standing team improving it every sprint, not the one with the biggest launch announcement.

AI agents will genuinely transform enterprise service delivery. But the winners won't be the companies with the best models. They'll be the ones that did the boring work underneath.
